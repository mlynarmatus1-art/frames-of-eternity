Christianity & Consciousness

How does Christianity, with a focus on Anglicanism, view consciousness?

In this video, we explore key Christian perspectives on consciousness with a focus on Anglicanism. Anglicanism does not preoccupy itself with defining consciousness in abstract terms; instead, it emphasises the process of sanctification and self-transformation. Drawing on the teachings of Wesley, Nissa, and John of the Cross, we see that as the self matures through spiritual practice, prayer, and sacramental participation, consciousness itself matures over time. Worship, sacraments, and liturgical rituals allow believers to engage both human and Divine time, fostering moral reflection and spiritual growth. In this way, Christian consciousness is understood not as a static state but as an ongoing process shaped by temporal experience and Divine interaction. While the human time is largely linear, marked by decay and change, the Divine time is not bound by the clocks. Thus, although Anglicanism delegates the definition of consciousness to philosophy and science, it engages with the formation and transformation of experienced self through acts, sacraments and the study of Scripture.

Answer to the Research Question:

In Christianity, and specifically Anglicanism, consciousness is not statically defined. Anglicanism values dialogue between clergy, laity and episcopal bodies - aiming for mutual growth and maturation. These discussions concede that some questions - such as those pertaining to consciousness - may require knowledge domains beyond theology. By participating in prayer, rituals, and sacramental life, believers experience both human and Divine time, allowing the self to develop and awareness to deepen. Consciousness is thus a dynamic process intertwined with spiritual growth and moral formation. The flow of experienced time provides space for spiritual growth and development through an ongoing process of sanctification.
